⚛️ Lecture 1: Newton's Laws — Getting Things Moving

Understanding forces, friction, and acceleration on a horizontal surface.

📝 The Big Question

"A 5 kg object is placed on a rough horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 30 N is applied. If the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.4, calculate the acceleration of the object."

🧠 What Physics Principle Is Hiding Here?

When you see an object, forces, and a request for acceleration, the first thing that should pop into your mind is Newton's Second Law of Motion!

🔑 Newton's Second Law (F = ma)

The net force acting on an object is directly proportional to its acceleration and in the same direction as the acceleration.

Fnet = ma, where:

  • Fnet = net force (vector sum of all forces)
  • m = mass of the object
  • a = acceleration of the object

🔑 Free Body Diagrams (FBD)

Visualize all forces acting on the object. This helps you:

  • Identify all forces (weight, normal, applied, friction)
  • Represent forces as vectors from the center of the object
  • Choose a coordinate system (x and y axes)

🔑 Friction (Kinetic)

Kinetic friction opposes ongoing motion. The formula is:

Ffriction = μk × N, where:

  • μk = coefficient of kinetic friction
  • N = normal force (on a horizontal surface, N = mg)

✏️ Solving the Problem

  1. Draw the Free Body Diagram: Weight (down), Normal (up), Applied Force (right), Friction (left).
  2. Vertical forces balance: N = mg = 5 × 9.8 = 49 N
  3. Calculate friction: Ffriction = 0.4 × 49 = 19.6 N
  4. Apply Newton's Second Law horizontally: Fapplied − Ffriction = ma
  5. Solve: 30 − 19.6 = 5a → a = 10.4 / 5 = 2.08 m/s²

✅ Final Answer: 2.08 m/s²
